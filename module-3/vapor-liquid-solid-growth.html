✨ In VLS growth, a catalyst (or impurity) forms a liquid droplet that traps growth species from the vapor; supersaturation leads to precipitation at the solid-liquid interface, enabling one-directional (nanowire) growth.
  • Principle: A second-phase material (catalyst) forms a liquid droplet by itself or by alloying with the growth material. The droplet acts as a preferential sink for vapor-phase species. When supersaturated, the growth species precipitate at the droplet-substrate interface, driving anisotropic crystal growth.

Requirements for VLS Growth

  • Liquid solution formation: The catalyst must form a liquid solution with the crystalline material at the deposition temperature.
  • Distribution coefficient < 1: The catalyst's distribution coefficient must be less than unity at the growth temperature to avoid incorporation into the solid.
  • Low vapor pressure of catalyst: The equilibrium vapor pressure over the liquid droplet must be very small to minimize catalyst evaporation.
  • Chemical inertness: The catalyst must be chemically inert toward the reaction environment and the growing crystal.
  • Interfacial energy & wetting: Wetting characteristics influence nanowire diameter, a small wetting angle yields a large growth area and larger diameter nanowire.
  • Self-catalysis for compounds: For compound nanowires, one of the constituents can serve as the catalyst (e.g., Ga in GaAs).
  • 7rystallographically defined interface: Controlled unidirectional growth requires a well-defined solid-liquid interface in crystallographic terms.
💠
Comparison: VLS vs. Vapor-Solid (VS)
In the VS process, the driving force is reduction in Gibbs free energy, leading to single-crystalline nanomaterials with fewer imperfections due to anisotropic growth. VLS, however, uses a liquid catalyst to confine and direct growth into nanowires.
