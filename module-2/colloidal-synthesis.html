✨ The synthesis of nanocrystals in colloidal solutions and the function of the colloidal template in controlling the size, shape and crystal properties of inorganic materials, as well as the various parameters affecting the size and shape.

Colloid

A colloidal system is a multiphase system in which at least one of the phases is composed of particles with sizes less than 1 μm. Many research methods have used reverse micelles (water droplets in oil, stabilized by a surfactant) to exercise very accurate control over the size of spherical nanoparticles.

Surfactant

Surfactants are molecules with a hydrophilic polar head and a hydrophobic hydrocarbon chain. Such molecules tend to spontaneously position themselves at the interface separating two immiscible media, such as water and oil, to form different aggregates.

CTAB in Water

CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) in water forms micelles that serve as soft templates, promoting anisotropic growth of nanomaterials via electrostatic and hydrophobic interactions.

SDS in Water

SDS (sodium dodecyl sulfate) in water adsorbs onto growing crystal surfaces through its anionic head group, modifying surface charge and directing particle shape and dispersion stability.

Removal of Surfactant

Surfactant removal is achieved either by solvent washing (dissolving the surfactant) or by thermal calcination (burning it off), both essential to expose clean, active surfaces of the synthesized product.

Hot Injection Method

Injection of reagents into the hot reaction vessel results in a short burst of homogeneous nucleation due to an abrupt supersaturation and simultaneously a sharp drop in temperature associated with the introduction of room temperature precursor solution.
The depletion of reagents through this nucleation burst prevents further nucleation events, while the remaining monomers continue to support controlled growth of the existing nuclei, typically leading to monodisperse nanoparticles.
Monodispersion is further achieved by gently reheating the solution to promote slow growth of initial nuclei. An increased temperature results in an increased solubility, and thus a reduced supersaturation of growth species in the solution.
As a result, nuclei with small sizes may become unstable and dissolve back into the solution; dissolved species will then deposit onto the surfaces of large particles. This dissolution-growth process is also known as Ostwald ripening, in which large particles grow at the expense of small particle.
Such a growth process would result in the production of highly monodispersed colloidal dispersions from systems that may initially be polydispersed.
Lowering the synthesis temperature results in a wider size distribution with an increased amount of small particles.
  • A lowered temperature would result in an increased supersaturation favoring continued nucleation with smaller sizes.
  • An increased temperature will promote the growth of nanoparticles with a narrow size distribution.
This method is used for metal materials due to their withstanding with high temperature.

Underlying Principle: Separation of Nucleation and Growth

  • Based on LaMer model
  • Rapid precursor decomposition increases monomer concentration
  • Burst nucleation occurs when supersaturation threshold is reached
  • Growth phase follows without formation of new nuclei

Experimental Setup

  • Three-neck round-bottom flask
  • Heating mantle or oil bath
  • Inert gas supply (\(N_2\) or \(Ar\))
  • Syringe for rapid injection
  • Magnetic stirring

Step-by-Step Procedure

  • Heat solvent and ligands to desired temperature
  • Prepare precursor solution separately
  • Rapidly inject precursor into hot solvent
  • Nucleation occurs immediately
  • Controlled growth of nanoparticles
  • Cool the reaction mixture

Parameters Affecting Nanoparticle Size

  • Reaction temperature: Higher temperature increases monomer decomposition rate and supersaturation, leading to more nuclei and smaller particles; lower temperature favors slower nucleation and larger particles.
  • Precursor concentration: Higher precursor concentration increases supersaturation, promoting burst nucleation and smaller particles; lower concentration yields fewer nuclei and larger final sizes.
  • Ligand concentration: Higher ligand concentration caps particle surfaces more effectively, limiting growth and producing smaller nanoparticles; lower ligand concentration allows unrestricted growth and larger sizes.
  • Injection speed: Faster injection creates a sharper supersaturation spike, resulting in more numerous nuclei and smaller particles; slower injection reduces nucleation burst, leading to larger, more polydisperse particles.
  • Growth time: Longer growth time allows Ostwald ripening and continued monomer consumption, generally increasing particle size until monomer depletion; shorter growth time yields smaller particles.

Advantages of the Hot Injection Method

  • Good control over particle size
  • Narrow size distribution
  • Reproducible synthesis
  • Suitable for many nanomaterials

Limitations of the Hot Injection Method

  • Requires high temperature solvents
  • Difficult to scale up
  • Requires inert atmosphere in many cases
  • Sensitive to injection speed and temperature

Formation of Micelle

  • (a) Surfactant with bulky polar head and short chain: The bulky head group increases the critical micelle concentration (CMC), favoring the formation of spherical micelles rather than cylindrical or lamellar structures.
  • (b) Surfactants join together: Hydrophobic tails aggregate via van der Waals forces to minimize contact with water, while hydrophilic heads remain exposed to the aqueous environment, driving self-assembly.
  • (c) A micelle forms: Once the CMC is exceeded, surfactants spontaneously organize into a core-shell structure with a hydrophobic interior and a hydrophilic exterior, enabling solubilization of nonpolar species.

Formation of a Reverse Micelle

  • (a) Surfactant with small polar head and ramified chain: The small polar head and branched hydrophobic chain promote the formation of reverse micelles in nonpolar solvents, where polar heads cluster inward to encapsulate water.
  • (b) Surfactants join together: In an oil-continuous phase, surfactants orient with their polar heads facing each other to shield water or polar molecules, driven by hydrophobic interactions and hydrogen bonding.
  • (c) A reverse micelle forms: The assembly creates a nanoscale water pool inside a surfactant shell dispersed in oil, enabling controlled synthesis of nanoparticles by confining reactions within the aqueous core.

Exchange of Aqueous Cores

  • (a) Reverse micelles subject to Brownian motion: Random collisions between reverse micelles in the oil phase bring their surfactant shells into contact, enabling temporary merging of the water pools.
  • (b) Collapsed reverse micelles and mixing of aqueous contents: During collision, the surfactant layers reorganize, allowing the encapsulated aqueous cores to fuse and exchange solutes (e.g., precursors, ions) before separation.
  • (c) Formation of two micelles, identical to the initial micelles: After exchange, the reverse micelles separate again, each containing a redistributed mixture of the original aqueous contents, enabling controlled nucleation and growth of nanoparticles.

Controlling the Size of Reverse Micelles by Water Content

The water-to-surfactant molar ratio (\(\omega\)) directly determines the size of the aqueous core in reverse micelles, enabling tunable nanoreactor dimensions for nanoparticle synthesis.
  • \(\omega = 2\) (low water content): Small reverse micelles form with tiny aqueous cores, restricting the volume for nucleation and leading to ultra-small nanoparticles.
  • \(\omega = 20\) (high water content): Larger reverse micelles swell with more water, creating bigger aqueous pools that can accommodate larger precursors and produce larger nanoparticles.
The water-to-surfactant molar ratio \(\omega\) controls the reverse micelle core size: higher \(\omega\) values yield larger aqueous pools and thus larger nanoparticle products.

✦
\(\omega\) = \([H_2O]\) \([\text{Surfactant Agent}]\)

Different Structures That Can Form in the Water-Oil-Surfactant Ternary System

  • Interconnected cylinders: Cylindrical micelles connect to form a network, enhancing viscosity and enabling confined reaction pathways for anisotropic nanoparticle growth.
  • Lamellar phase: Alternating surfactant bilayers separated by water or oil layers create a one-dimensional periodic structure, useful for templating nanosheets and layered materials.
  • Superaggregate: Higher-order clustering of micelles or vesicles occurs near phase boundaries, leading to porous or hierarchical architectures that influence mass transport and nucleation sites.

Difference between reverse micellar and Hot-injection method

The hot injection method and reverse micellar synthesis are both colloidal techniques but differ fundamentally in their approach. Hot injection involves rapidly injecting precursors into a hot solvent, causing instantaneous nucleation followed by controlled growth at elevated temperatures (150-300 \(^\circ\)C), yielding highly monodisperse nanoparticles. Reverse micellar synthesis uses water-in-oil micelles as nanoreactors, where precursors are confined within aqueous droplets; nucleation and growth occur at room temperature or mild heating, with particle size controlled by the water-to-surfactant molar ratio (w). Hot injection produces superior size uniformity, while reverse micelles offer better scalability and milder conditions.

Reverse Micelles: Spherical Nanoreactors

💠
Reaction Methodology
Two reactants A and B are solubilized separately in two micelle solutions. When the two solutions are mixed, aqueous core exchange induced by Brownian motion brings A and B into contact so that they can react to give products. After reaction, nanocrystals can be extracted from the system.

Effect of Micelles on Size, Shape, and Crystallinity of Nanoparticles

💠
Size
The micelle acts as a physical boundary for the reaction, and the diameter of the micellar core directly governs the final particle size. Since nucleation and growth are confined within this nanoscale reactor, the particle cannot grow beyond the dimensions of the micelle. Adjusting the surfactant concentration, the water-to-surfactant ratio in reverse micellar systems (\(\omega\) = [H₂O]/[SA]), or the tail length of the surfactant all shift the micellar diameter and consequently the mean nanoparticle size. For example, silver nanocrystals with average diameters of 5.2 nm and 6.3 nm were obtained simply by changing \(\omega\).
💠
Shape
Shape is influenced by the geometry of the micellar aggregate. Spherical micelles template roughly isotropic particles, while rod-like or worm-like micelles direct anisotropic growth, favoring elongated morphologies. The packing parameter of the surfactant (depending on head group area, tail volume, and tail length) determines which aggregate geometry is preferred. CTAB is a well-known example where, at appropriate concentrations, it promotes gold nanorod formation by selectively stabilizing certain crystal facets. Copper nanocrystals synthesized in different phases (e.g., interconnected cylinders, lamellar, superaggregates) of the water/surfactant/oil system exhibit distinct shapes.
💠
Crystallinity
The confinement within the micelle tends to favor fewer nucleation events and more ordered crystal growth compared to bulk synthesis. Surfactant molecules at the micellar interface can passivate specific crystallographic facets, influencing whether the particle develops as a single crystal or acquires twinning defects. The reduction rate and temperature within the micelle further modulate the degree of crystalline order. The key to obtaining well-crystallized nanocrystals often lies in the use of functionalized surfactants. For instance, triangular CdS nanocrystals have been obtained using a reverse micelle system.

Synthesis of Silver Nanomaterials

5 nm nanospheres and nanorods of aspect ratio 2 (length ~10 nm, width ~5 nm) via reverse micelle templating.
  • Given:
    • Precursor: Silver Nitrate (\(AgNO_3\))
💠
Reverse Micelle Design Strategy
To obtain both nanospheres and nanorods, we use a water-in-oil microemulsion stabilized by a surfactant. Size is controlled by the water-to-surfactant molar ratio (\(\omega\) = [H₂O]/[surfactant]), and shape is directed by the surfactant packing parameter and the addition of a co-surfactant that promotes cylindrical micelles.

Typical Chemicals Used

  • Silver precursor: Silver Nitrate (\(AgNO_3\))
  • Reducing agent: Sodium borohydride (\(NaBH_4\)) or hydrazine (N₂H₄)
  • Oil phase: Isooctane or cyclohexane
  • Surfactant (for spheres): AOT (sodium bis(2-ethylhexyl)sulfosuccinate)
  • Co-surfactant (for rods): CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) or Brij-58
  • Aqueous phase: Deionized water
Silver nitrate (\(AgNO_3\)) is chosen as a soluble silver ion source; \(NaBH_4\) provides rapid, strong reduction for burst nucleation; isooctane serves as a low-polarity oil phase to stabilize reverse micelles; AOT forms spherical micelles for isotropic 5-nm spheres, while CTAB acts as a co-surfactant to increase the packing parameter and promote cylindrical micelles that template nanorods with aspect ratio 2.

Experimental Setup

  • Two separate flasks for precursor and reducing agent solutions
  • Magnetic stirrers for each solution
  • Syringe or pipette for mixing the two microemulsions
  • Centrifuge for nanoparticle extraction
  • Ultrasonic bath (optional for dispersion)

Step-by-Step Procedure

  • Prepare reverse micelle solution A (silver precursor): Dissolve \(AgNO_3\) in deionized water. Add this aqueous solution to a mixture of isooctane and AOT surfactant to achieve \(\omega = \frac{[H₂O]}{[AOT]} = 4\) (for 5 nm spheres) or \(\omega\) = 10 (for slightly larger cores if needed). Stir until clear.
  • Prepare reverse micelle solution B (reducing agent): Dissolve \(NaBH_4\) in deionized water. Mix with isooctane and AOT at the same \(\omega\) value as solution A. For nanorods, add CTAB as a co-surfactant (surfactant packing parameter > 1/3 to promote cylindrical micelles).
  • Equilibrate both solutions: Stir each solution under inert atmosphere (N₂ or Ar) for 15 minutes to allow reverse micelle formation.
  • Mix the two microemulsions: Under vigorous stirring, slowly add solution B to solution A (or inject rapidly for burst nucleation). Brownian motion causes collision and fusion of reverse micelles, leading to exchange of aqueous cores.
  • Reaction and nucleation: Ag⁺ ions meet \(NaBH_4\) in the fused water pool and reduce to Ag⁰ atoms. Nucleation occurs within the confined micellar core.
  • Growth and shape evolution: For spheres (using AOT only), growth remains isotropic, yielding ~5 nm particles. For rods (with CTAB), CTAB adsorbs selectively on the {100} facets of silver, directing anisotropic growth along the <110> direction to achieve aspect ratio 2.
  • Quench and extraction: Add acetone or ethanol to break the reverse micelles, then centrifuge to collect the silver nanoparticles. Wash with ethanol to remove excess surfactant and oil.
💠
Key Parameters for Size and Shape Control
  • For 5 nm nanospheres: \(\omega = 4\), pure AOT surfactant (packing parameter ~0.3 → spherical micelles), reduction with \(NaBH_4\) at \(25^{\circ}C\).
  • For nanorods (aspect ratio 2): \(\omega = 6\), AOT + CTAB (CTAB increases packing parameter > 0.5 → cylindrical micelles), reduction at \(40^{\circ}C\) with a mild reducer (e.g., ascorbic acid) after initial burst with \(NaBH_4\).

Synthesis of Gold Nanomaterials

Spherical gold nanoparticles with tunable size (e.g., 5-20 nm) via reverse micelle templating, using CTAB as the cationic surfactant.
  • Given:
    • Precursor: Chloroauric acid (\(HAuCl_4\))

Typical Chemicals Used

  • Gold precursor: Chloroauric acid (\(HAuCl_4\))
  • Reducing agent: Sodium borohydride (NaBH₄) or mild reducers (e.g., oleylamine)
  • Solvent: High-boiling organic solvents (octadecene, oleylamine) or water (for reverse micelles)
  • Surfactant: CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) - cationic head group
  • Oil phase (for reverse micelle): Isooctane or cyclohexane
  • Co-surfactant (optional): Brij-58 or oleic acid

Experimental Setup

  • Two separate flasks for precursor and reducing agent microemulsions
  • Magnetic stirrers for each solution
  • Syringe or pipette for mixing the two microemulsions
  • Centrifuge for nanoparticle extraction
  • Ultrasonic bath (optional for dispersion)

Step-by-Step Procedure

  • Prepare reverse micelle solution A (gold precursor): Dissolve \(HAuCl_4\) in deionized water. Add this aqueous solution to a mixture of isooctane and CTAB surfactant to achieve \(\omega = \frac{[H₂O]}{[CTAB]} = 5-10\). Stir until clear. The cationic CTAB head attracts the anionic \(AuCl₄^{-}\) into the water pool.
  • Prepare reverse micelle solution B (reducing agent): Dissolve NaBH₄ in deionized water. Mix with isooctane and CTAB at the same ω value as solution A.
  • Equilibrate both solutions: Stir each under inert atmosphere (\(N_2\) or \(Ar\)) for 15 min to allow reverse micelle formation.
  • Mix the two microemulsions: Under vigorous stirring, slowly add solution B to solution A. Brownian motion causes collision and fusion of reverse micelles, leading to exchange of aqueous cores.
  • Reduction and nucleation: Within the fused water pool, NaBH₄ reduces Au³⁺ to Au⁰ atoms. Burst nucleation occurs, confined by the micelle.
  • Growth and stabilization: The CTAB molecules passivate the gold surface, preventing agglomeration. Particle size is determined by the micelle core diameter (controlled by ω).
  • Quench and extraction: Add acetone or ethanol to break the reverse micelles, then centrifuge to collect gold nanoparticles. Wash with ethanol to remove excess surfactant and oil.
💠
Design Strategy: Why CTAB and How Reduction Works
Chloroauric acid (\(HAuCl_4\)) dissociates in water into H⁺ and AuCl₄⁻, making the solution acidic. For reverse micelle templating, the surfactant head group must be positively charged to attract the anionic AuCl₄⁻. CTAB (cetyltrimethylammonium bromide) provides a permanent positive charge on its quaternary ammonium head, enabling effective sequestration of the gold complex inside the water pool.

When NaBH₄ is added, it donates electrons to reduce Au³⁺ (in AuCl₄⁻) down to Au⁰, which nucleates and grows into gold nanoparticles. The byproducts are NaCl, water, and boric acid (H₃BO₃). The micelle acts as a nanoscale reaction vessel, confining particle growth to its interior dimensions - therefore the final nanoparticle size is dictated largely by the micelle geometry (ω = [H₂O]/[CTAB]).

Gold Nanoparticle Applications

  • Unique optical properties (surface plasmon resonance)
  • Applications in sensing, catalysis, medicine, and electronics
  • Size-dependent physical and chemical properties
  • Widely studied model nanomaterial
