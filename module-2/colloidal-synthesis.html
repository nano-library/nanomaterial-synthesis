Hot Injection Method

Injection of reagents into the hot reaction vessel results in a short burst of homogeneous nucleation due to an abrupt supersaturation and simultaneously a sharp drop in temperature associated with the introduction of room temperature precursor solution.
The depletion of reagents through such nucleation prevents further nucleation and also largely hinders the subsequent growth of existing nuclei.
Monodispersion is hrther achieved by gently reheating the solution to promote slow growth of initial nuclei. An increased temperature results in an increased solubility, and thus a reduced supersaturation of growth species in the solution.
As a result, nuclei with small sizes may become unstable and dissolve back into the solution; dissolved species will then deposit onto the surfaces of large particles. This dissolution-growth process is also known as Ostwald ripening, in which large particles grow at the expense of small particle.
Such a growth process would result in the production of highly monodispersed colloidal dispersions from systems that may initially be polydispersed.
Lowering the synthesis temperature results in a wider size distribution with an increased amount of small particles. A lowered temperature would result in an increased supersaturation favoring continued nucleation with smaller sizes. An increased temperature will promote the growth of nanoparticles with a narrow size distribution.
This method is used for metal materials due to their withstanding with high temperature.

Gold Nanoparticle Synthesis

Chloroauric acid (\(HAuCl_4\)) dissociates in water into \(H^⁺\) and \(AuCl^-_4\), making the solution acidic. When sodium borohydride (\(NaBH_4\)) is added as a reducing agent, it donates electrons to reduce the \(Au^{3+}\) in \(AuCl^⁻_4\) down to \(Au^0\), which nucleates and grows into gold nanoparticles. The byproducts are \(NaCl\), water, and boric acid (\(H_3BO_3\)).

Gold Nanoparticle Applications

  • Unique optical properties (surface plasma resonance)
  • Applications in sensing, catalysis, medicine, and electronics.
  • Size-Dependent physical and chemical properties.
  • Widely studied model nanomaterial.
The micelle in this system acts as a nanoscale reaction vessel, confining particle growth to its interior dimensions and thereby controlling the final nanoparticle size. For this templating approach to work, the surfactant head group must be positively charged. The gold precursor, \(\text{AuCl}_4^-\), is anionic, So a cationic head group is required to attract and sequester it within the micelle. A negatively charged head group would repel \(\text{AuCl}_4^-\) and defeat the purpose entirely. CTAB is the classic example of a suitable surfactant here, with its permanent positive charge on the quaternary ammonium head group enabling effective interaction with the gold complex. Reduction then occurs within the confined micellar space, and the particle size is dictated largely by the micelle geometry.

Difference between reverse micellar and Hot-injection method

The hot injection method and reverse micellar synthesis are both colloidal techniques but differ fundamentally in their approach. Hot injection involves rapidly injecting precursors into a hot solvent, causing instantaneous nucleation followed by controlled growth at elevated temperatures (150-300 \(^\circ\)C), yielding highly monodisperse nanoparticles. Reverse micellar synthesis uses water-in-oil micelles as nanoreactors, where precursors are confined within aqueous droplets; nucleation and growth occur at room temperature or mild heating, with particle size controlled by the water-to-surfactant molar ratio (w). Hot injection produces superior size uniformity, while reverse micelles offer better scalability and milder conditions.

3.1

Effect of Micelles on Size, Shape, and Crystallinity of Nanoparticles

💠
Size
The micelle acts as a physical boundary for the reaction, and the diameter of the micellar core directly governs the final particle size. Since nucleation and growth are confined within this nanoscale reactor, the particle cannot grow beyond the dimensions of the micelle. Adjusting the surfactant concentration, the water-to-surfactant ratio in reverse micellar systems, or the tail length of the surfactant all shift the micellar diameter and consequently the mean nanoparticle size.
💠
Shape
Shape is influenced by the geometry of the micellar aggregate. Spherical micelles template roughly isotropic particles, while rod-like or worm-like micelles direct anisotropic growth, favoring elongated morphologies. The packing parameter of the surfactant, which depends on head group area, tail volume, and tail length, determines which aggregate geometry is preferred. CTAB is a well-known example where, at appropriate concentrations, it promotes gold nanorod formation by selectively stabilizing certain crystal facets through preferential adsorption.
💠
Crystallinity
The confinement within the micelle tends to favor fewer nucleation events and more ordered crystal growth compared to bulk synthesis. Surfactant molecules at the micellar interface can passivate specific crystallographic facets, influencing whether the particle develops as a single crystal or acquires twinning defects. The reduction rate and temperature within the micelle further modulate the degree of crystalline order in the final particle.
